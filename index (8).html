Tire rubber is vulcanized — its polymer chains are permanently cross-linked with sulfur into a 3D network. That makes it a thermoset, not a thermoplastic. Unlike a milk jug, you can't heat it back into a liquid and re-flow it into a new shape.

So "crumb rubber molding" almost never means melting recycled rubber. It means gluing millions of granules together with a binder, then pressing and curing that binder into a solid mass. The rubber particles are the aggregate; the binder is the cement.

01The feedstock

It starts as a worn-out tire

Roughly a billion tires reach end-of-life every year. A passenger tire is mostly recoverable material once you separate the three things bonded inside it: rubber, steel, and textile fiber.

Whole tires are first shredded into chips. From there the goal is clean rubber granules — which means pulling out the steel belts and the fabric reinforcement before anything gets molded.

02Crumb production

Grinding tires into graded crumb

Two grinding routes dominate. Ambient grinding shears rubber at room temperature, producing rough, irregular particles — the extra surface area grabs adhesive well. Cryogenic grinding freezes the rubber with liquid nitrogen until it's brittle, then shatters it into cleaner, finer particles.

The crumb is screened and sold by particle size, measured in mesh. Coarse granules fill the structural body of a part; fine crumb gives a smooth surface and colored wear layers.

03Mix with binder

Polyurethane is the glue

The crumb is tumbled in a mixer with a liquid binder — typically a moisture-curing, MDI-based polyurethane prepolymer. Pigments or colored EPDM granules can join here for surface layers. The result looks like damp gravel: every particle wears a thin coat of adhesive.

The ratio is the first thing that decides quality. Too little binder and the part crumbles; too much wastes material and deadens the rubbery feel. Most products land around 8–12% binder by weight.

04Mold & cure

Pressure sets the shape, cure locks it

The damp mix is loaded into a mold cavity and compressed to a target density. Then the binder has to set — and there are two routes, both shown in the drums and presses you uploaded.

Hot compression molding: heated platens close on the mold, curing the polyurethane in minutes. Fast cycles, higher throughput, dense parts — the route behind pavers, wheel stops, and gym tiles.

05Finished product

Out of the mold, into service

The cured part is ejected, sometimes post-cured to fully harden the binder, then trimmed and stacked. Many products use a clever two-layer build: a thick, cheap black recycled base topped with a thin layer of colored EPDM granules for a clean, UV-stable wearing surface — molded in a single shot.

Worth knowing

Bound crumb isn’t the only path

Everything above is "bound crumb" — particles glued into shape. There's a separate, harder chemistry that actually undoes vulcanization.

What you saw here

Bound crumb

Granules held together by a polyurethane binder under pressure and cure. The rubber itself is never chemically changed.

  • Simple, low energy, widely used
  • Makes tiles, pavers, mats, bumpers
  • Binder is the structural element
The harder route

Devulcanization

Heat, chemicals, ultrasound, or microwaves break the sulfur cross-links so the rubber can be re-compounded into new rubber goods.

  • Restores some processability
  • Feeds back into new rubber compounds
  • More complex and energy-intensive
